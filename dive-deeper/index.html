Dive Deeper

In real-world open-source projects, there's a ton of essential documents for a project, from contributor codes of conducts to style guides.

However, for the purposes of a quickly-built hackathon project, we can start with just a few pieces of general documentation:

  • README
  • How-Tos
  • Overview

README

If there's any type of documentation that you're likely familiar with, it's this one. It's your project's first impression, and is crucial to enticing users to explore further.

A good README:

  • Provides an introduction to what the project is, why it may be relevant to the reader, and how they can learn more
  • Is written in an approachable and friendly style, inviting the reader in
  • Utilizes an active voice with strong verbs, selling the reader (who could be a hackathon judge or future employer) on it
  • Focuses on the value the project brings to a user, not just what it can do

Some Resources:

  • The Good Docs Project's README guide
  • Daniel Beck's README Checklist
  • Matias Singers's list of good README examples

How-Tos

An important type of document for projects, even small ones, to have is "how-tos" for essential tasks. One that every project should have is "how to set up a development environment for {project}". Other good ones may include:

  • "How to deploy {project}'s backend"
  • "How to create an account on {project}"
  • "How to run {project} in debugging mode"

Note that a how-to and a tutorial are different. How-tos focus on helping a technically proficient user accomplish a certain task effectively, while tutorials focus on teaching a beginner something new. At this stage of a project, a few good how-tos are helpful, not only for anybody trying to use or judge your project, but also for yourself when you sit down to work on improvements in a few weeks and remember nothing about how your project works internally.

A good how-to:

  • Focuses on accomplishing a single, distinct task
  • Assumes that users have practical knowledge of surrounding technologies (to keep things concise) and states these assumptions at the beginning.
  • Alerts the user to any problems they may run into along the way

Some Resources:

  • The Good Docs Project's guide on writing a how-to

Overview

While not many small open-source projects usually have a dedicated overview document, in the context of a hackathon, it makes a lot of sense.

An overview is a document that gives a broad outline of your project's features and capabilities. Importantly, it pitches the reader on why they should care. Writing an overview, and being able to draw on that as a resource to market and talk about your project, is crucial to ensuring everyone is on the same page about why what you've built is important and innovative.

A good overview:

  • Is written with a distinct audience in mind
    • Who is this project intended for? Is it someone in a certain role? Somebody with a certain problem to solve?
  • Has clear and concise explanations of your project's key functions
  • Makes a strong case for why the project is important to the reader

Some Resources:

  • The Good Docs Project's overview guide
  • A great overview example for Google's Native Client technology

With just these three pieces of documentation, you'll substantially increase how much potential users, hackathon judges, and future you will understand your project. Don't feel limited to this set, however! While time is precious in a hackathon setting, if there's another type of documentation that makes sense to add, quickly write it up. It'll be a nice break for your brain, may unlock further inspiration or insights, and that information will likely come in handy in the future.
